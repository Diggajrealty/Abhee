RERA Demystified: The Ultimate Buyer's Checklist

Published on May 2, 2026 | By The Legal Advisory Team

Executive Summary

Before 2016, buying an under-construction property in India was like playing Russian roulette with your life savings. Builders could indefinitely delay projects, funnel your money into other ventures, and leave you paying both rent and EMIs. Then came RERA (The Real Estate Regulation and Development Act). This article strips away the heavy legal jargon to explain exactly how RERA protects you, the ingenious mechanism of the escrow account, and the simple checklist you must follow before signing any property agreement.

1. The Dark Ages: Real Estate Before 2016

Let's have some real talk about how bad things used to be. If you were buying property prior to 2016, the system was heavily rigged against you. A common strategy among shady developers was to launch a project, collect 20% to 30% of the funds from enthusiastic buyers during "pre-launch," and then use that exact money to buy land for their *next* project.

It was a massive Ponzi scheme. When funding dried up, construction simply stopped. Buyers were left stranded, fighting court battles that lasted decades, while simultaneously paying rent for their current homes and EMIs for an apartment that was just a muddy hole in the ground. I personally knew families whose financial futures were completely derailed by unscrupulous builders who promised the moon and delivered nothing.

There was no central authority to complain to. It was the wild west. Then, the government finally stepped in and fundamentally changed the industry overnight.

2. What Exactly is RERA?

RERA stands for the Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act, passed in 2016. Think of it as the ultimate referee for the real estate industry. Its sole purpose is to protect the homebuyer and ensure absolute transparency in the buying process.

Under RERA, it is illegal for a developer to advertise, market, book, sell, or even invite persons to purchase any plot, apartment, or building without first registering the project with the state's RERA authority (in our case, Karnataka RERA or K-RERA). To get this registration number, the builder has to submit practically every detail of the project: the exact land titles, all civic approvals, detailed layout plans, and strict timelines for completion.

If a builder defaults on these promised timelines, the penalties are severe. They have to pay heavy interest to the buyers for every month of delay. This simple rule cleaned up the market almost instantly, weeding out the fly-by-night operators and leaving only serious, financially stable developers in the game.

3. The Magic of the 70% Escrow Account

This is arguably the most brilliant and protective clause in the entire RERA act. Remember the Ponzi scheme issue I mentioned earlier, where builders used your money to fund different projects?

RERA completely killed that practice by introducing the mandatory Escrow Account. Hereâ€™s how it works: When you pay your installment to the builder, 70% of that money must be deposited into a separate, dedicated bank account (an escrow account) that is strictly linked to *that specific project*. The builder cannot touch this money for anything else.

They can only withdraw funds from this account to cover the cost of construction and land for that project, and they can only do so after it is certified by an engineer, an architect, and a chartered accountant in practice. This guarantees that your hard-earned money is actually being used to lay the bricks of your future home, ensuring construction moves swiftly and predictably.

4. The "Carpet Area" Revolution

Before RERA, builders used a highly deceptive metric called "Super Built-up Area" to sell apartments. They would quote you a massive square footage, but that number included the lobby, the staircase, the elevator shafts, and sometimes even a proportionate share of the clubhouse.

When you actually got the keys and walked into the apartment, it felt suspiciously small. Thatâ€™s because you were paying for common areas. RERA stopped this nonsense dead in its tracks. The act mandates that developers can only quote prices based on the "Carpet Area."

The carpet area is exactly what it sounds like: the actual usable floor area of an apartment where you can lay a carpet. It excludes the thickness of the external walls, service shafts, balconies, and common areas. This brings absolute transparency to pricing, allowing you to compare Project A against Project B fairly, apples to apples.

6. Frequently Asked Questions

How can I check if a project is RERA registered?

It's very easy. Go to the official Karnataka RERA website (rera.karnataka.gov.in). You can search for the project using its name, the promoter's name, or the specific RERA registration number provided in the builder's marketing materials.

Does RERA cover structural defects?

Yes! Under RERA, if any structural defect or defect in workmanship is brought to the notice of the promoter within a period of 5 years from the date of handing over possession, the promoter must rectify the defect without further charge within 30 days.

Is RERA applicable to commercial properties?

Absolutely. The RERA act covers both residential and commercial real estate projects, providing the exact same protections to shop owners and office space buyers as it does to homebuyers.
